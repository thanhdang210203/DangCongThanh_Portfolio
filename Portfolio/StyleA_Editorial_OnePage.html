Thất bại là mẹ thành công

A Vietnamese saying that shaped how I approach every design problem. Not a phrase — a working method. Every iteration that fails teaches something a successful one cannot.

I design by interrogating what broke, not celebrating what worked.

My name, Công Thành, inverts the Vietnamese word for success — Thành Công. That contrast reflects how I think. Progress in design, as in most difficult disciplines, is built from effort, missteps, and honest reflection — not from getting things right the first time.

At VigaFun I learned that strong design decisions are almost never about adding features. They are about identifying what creates friction and removing it. The most impactful change I made on a shipped title was not a new mechanic — it was adjusting when advertisements appeared. Retention improved. The data made the argument; I just read it correctly.

VigaFun · July 2025 – Present

Color Knitzy

Published Mobile Game · Available on iOS & Android

A colour-based matching mobile title where I carried full responsibility for level architecture, mechanic specification, and feature iteration. My work spanned the complete design pipeline: from initial mechanic documentation through to difficulty-curve balancing using live retention data. Every level was designed against a specific player-skill hypothesis, tested, and revised based on behavioural patterns observed in production analytics.

↳ Key Design Decision & Outcome

Structured playtest analysis revealed that player drop-off was concentrated not around mechanic difficulty — which was functioning within expected parameters — but around advertisement pacing interrupting player flow states. Rather than redesigning the mechanic, I proposed restructuring the timing and placement of ad events within the session arc. Retention metrics improved in subsequent live updates. The lesson: friction is almost always systemic, not mechanical.

LacBird.co · Dec 2023 – Apr 2024

Paper Flower

Narrative Game · LacBird.co Studio

My first professional project — a narrative-driven game where I served as narrative game designer and, unexpectedly, lead designer and project coordinator for a four-person team. This is the project that clarified the gap between capability and preparation. Scope definition fell apart. Production structure was absent. Cross-role communication broke down. The project stalled under my leadership. Understanding precisely why that happened changed every decision I have made since.

↳ Turning Point

I stopped treating that failure as the team's problem the moment I recognised it was a leadership and preparation failure. Taking full ownership of it — rather than distributing the blame — was the most significant professional development moment of my early career. Every project management and documentation habit I now have traces back to what I learned from this one not working.

Designing the underlying structures that connect mechanics, player psychology, and experience goals. Breaking down why existing systems function, then specifying new ones. Difficulty architecture, feature interdependency mapping, and player learning trajectory design.

Level Design

Building spaces and sequences that communicate with players without exposition. Cross-context experience spanning mobile constraints and narrative environments. Balancing spatial composition, cognitive pacing, and challenge calibration at the level-by-level scale.

◎

Narrative Integration

Constructing story content that operates in service of mechanics rather than alongside them. Experience designing narrative that respects player agency, responds to gameplay states, and creates emotional context for mechanical choices without interrupting the play loop.

▤

Game Design Documentation

Authoring GDDs that function as living production references — not ceremonial documents. Mechanic specifications, feature briefs, iteration changelogs, and system dependency maps written so that engineers and artists can act on them without reinterpretation.

◐

Playtesting & Iteration

Structured observation protocols, live data analysis, and hypothesis-driven iteration cycles. Not adjusting designs from intuition — building and testing a specific claim about player behaviour, recording the result, and making the next decision from that evidence.

△

Creative Direction & Film

Direction, cinematography, and narrative structure across short film and trailer work. Cross-medium experience that directly informs how I think about player experience pacing, atmosphere construction, and the craft of controlling attention across a designed timeline.

The games industry I work in is shaped by mobile market constraints — short session windows, aggressive monetisation targets, and player bases with extremely low friction tolerance. I understand those constraints from the inside. Working within them has made me a more disciplined designer. But discipline applied to a constrained context eventually hits a ceiling.

The next stage requires operating in an environment where design decisions are tested not against retention dashboards alone, but against the full weight of craft, research, and production rigour. That shift in context — from surviving constraints to setting standards — is what drives the direction I am heading.

The goal is not a different geography. It is a higher level of practice — one where I can contribute to shaping what serious game design looks like in a market that is only beginning to ask that question.
